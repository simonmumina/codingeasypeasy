# The Pythagorean Theorem Explained: Formula, Proof, Applications & Examples

The Pythagorean Theorem, a cornerstone of geometry, is a fundamental relationship between the sides of a **right triangle**. It's a concept that pops up everywhere from basic math problems to complex engineering designs. In this comprehensive guide, we'll break down the Pythagorean Theorem, explore its formula, delve into its proofs, showcase its diverse applications, and provide clear examples to solidify your understanding.

## What is the Pythagorean Theorem?

At its core, the Pythagorean Theorem states: **In a right triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides (the legs).**

Think of it this way: imagine you have a right triangle.  If you were to build a square on each side of the triangle, the area of the square on the hypotenuse would be equal to the combined area of the squares on the other two sides.

## The Pythagorean Theorem Formula

The theorem is expressed mathematically as:

**a² + b² = c²**

Where:

*   **a** and **b** are the lengths of the two legs (the sides that form the right angle).
*   **c** is the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle).

## Visualizing the Theorem

It's often helpful to visualize the theorem.  Imagine a right triangle with legs of length 3 and 4.  The hypotenuse would then be of length 5.  Let's illustrate:

*   a = 3
*   b = 4
*   c = 5

Applying the formula:

3² + 4² = 5²

9 + 16 = 25

25 = 25

This confirms the theorem holds true.

## Proofs of the Pythagorean Theorem

There are numerous proofs of the Pythagorean Theorem, demonstrating its validity from various angles. Here are a couple of classic examples:

### 1. Geometric Proof

This proof involves arranging four identical right triangles within a larger square in two different ways.

*   **Arrangement 1:** Four triangles surrounding a smaller square. The area of the larger square is (a+b)².  The area of the smaller square is c².  The area of the four triangles is 4 * (1/2 * a * b) = 2ab. Therefore, (a+b)² = c² + 2ab.

*   **Arrangement 2:**  Four triangles surrounding two squares of area a² and b². The area of the larger square is still (a+b)².  The area of the two squares is a² + b².  The area of the four triangles is again 2ab. Therefore, (a+b)² = a² + b² + 2ab.

Since both arrangements describe the same area, we can equate the two equations:

c² + 2ab = a² + b² + 2ab

Subtracting 2ab from both sides, we arrive at:

c² = a² + b²

### 2. Algebraic Proof

This proof relies on manipulating algebraic expressions. Consider the arrangement mentioned above in geometric proof 1: Four right triangles surrounding a smaller square.

The area of the larger square is (a + b)². Expanding this, we get:

(a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²

The area of the larger square can also be represented as the sum of the areas of the four triangles and the smaller square:

Area = 4 * (1/2 * a * b) + c² = 2ab + c²

Equating the two expressions for the area of the larger square:

a² + 2ab + b² = 2ab + c²

Subtracting 2ab from both sides, we again get:

a² + b² = c²

## Applications of the Pythagorean Theorem

The Pythagorean Theorem isn't just a theoretical concept; it has widespread practical applications in various fields:

*   **Construction:** Calculating the length of rafters for a roof or determining the dimensions of a rectangular building.
*   **Navigation:** Calculating distances and bearings, especially in aviation and maritime navigation.
*   **Engineering:** Designing bridges, buildings, and other structures, ensuring stability and safety.
*   **Surveying:** Determining distances and elevations of land features.
*   **Computer Graphics:** Calculating distances and lengths in 3D graphics and game development.

## Examples of Using the Pythagorean Theorem

Let's work through some examples to illustrate how to use the theorem:

**Example 1: Finding the Hypotenuse**

A right triangle has legs of length 6 and 8. Find the length of the hypotenuse.

*   a = 6
*   b = 8
*   c = ?

Using the formula:

a² + b² = c²

6² + 8² = c²

36 + 64 = c²

100 = c²

c = √100 = 10

The length of the hypotenuse is 10.

**Example 2: Finding a Leg**

A right triangle has a hypotenuse of length 13 and one leg of length 5. Find the length of the other leg.

*   a = 5
*   b = ?
*   c = 13

Using the formula:

a² + b² = c²

5² + b² = 13²

25 + b² = 169

b² = 169 - 25

b² = 144

b = √144 = 12

The length of the other leg is 12.

**Example 3: Real-World Application**

A 15-foot ladder is leaning against a wall. The base of the ladder is 5 feet away from the wall. How high up the wall does the ladder reach?

*   a = 5 (distance from the wall)
*   b = ? (height on the wall)
*   c = 15 (length of the ladder)

Using the formula:

a² + b² = c²

5² + b² = 15²

25 + b² = 225

b² = 225 - 25

b² = 200

b = √200 ≈ 14.14

The ladder reaches approximately 14.14 feet up the wall.

## Code Examples (JavaScript)

Here's a simple JavaScript function to calculate the hypotenuse of a right triangle:

```javascript
function calculateHypotenuse(a, b) {
  const aSquared = a * a;
  const bSquared = b * b;
  const cSquared = aSquared + bSquared;
  const c = Math.sqrt(cSquared);
  return c;
}

console.log(calculateHypotenuse(3, 4)); // Output: 5
console.log(calculateHypotenuse(6, 8)); // Output: 10
```

Here's a function to calculate a missing leg given the hypotenuse and the other leg:

```javascript
function calculateLeg(c, a) {
    const cSquared = c * c;
    const aSquared = a * a;
    const bSquared = cSquared - aSquared;
    const b = Math.sqrt(bSquared);
    return b;
}

console.log(calculateLeg(5, 3)); // Output: 4
console.log(calculateLeg(13, 5)); // Output: 12
```

## Pythagorean Triples

A Pythagorean triple is a set of three positive integers a, b, and c, such that a² + b² = c². The most common example is (3, 4, 5).  Other examples include (5, 12, 13), (8, 15, 17), and (7, 24, 25).

These triples are particularly useful in construction and design because they represent whole number measurements for right triangles, simplifying calculations.
