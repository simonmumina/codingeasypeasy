## Puzzle 1: The Classic Coin Problem

**The Puzzle:**

You have 12 coins, and you know that one of them is counterfeit. The counterfeit coin is either heavier or lighter than the genuine coins. You have a balance scale, but you can only use it three times. How can you identify the counterfeit coin and determine whether it's heavier or lighter?

**Solution:**

This is a classic puzzle that demonstrates the power of methodical deduction. Here's the solution:

1.  **Weighing 1:** Divide the 12 coins into three groups of four: A, B, and C.  Place group A on one side of the scale and group B on the other.

    *   **Case 1: The scale balances.**  This means the counterfeit coin is in group C (the unweighed group). Proceed to Weighing 2 with group C.
    *   **Case 2: The scale tips.** This means the counterfeit coin is in either group A or group B. Proceed to Weighing 2 with groups A and B.

2.  **Weighing 2 (Case 1: Counterfeit in Group C):**  Take three coins from group C and weigh them against three known genuine coins (you can temporarily remove coins from group A or B if necessary, assuming they are genuine).

    *   **Case 1a: The scale balances.** This means the remaining coin from group C is the counterfeit. Weigh it against a known genuine coin in Weighing 3 to determine if it's heavier or lighter.
    *   **Case 1b: The scale tips.** This means one of the three coins being weighed is the counterfeit.  Weigh two of these against each other in Weighing 3 to identify the counterfeit and whether it's heavier or lighter.

3.  **Weighing 2 (Case 2: Counterfeit in Group A or B):** Take three coins from the *lighter* side of the scale in Weighing 1 (let's assume group A was lighter) and weigh them against three coins from the *heavier* side of the scale in Weighing 1 (group B).

    *   **Case 2a: The scale balances.** This means the counterfeit coin is one of the two coins from the *lighter* side (A) or *heavier* side (B) of the scale that were *not* included in this weighing. Weigh those two coins against each other in Weighing 3 to determine the counterfeit and whether it's heavier or lighter.
    *   **Case 2b: The scale tips (towards A, the original lighter side).** This means the counterfeit coin is one of the three original 'lighter' coins (from group A) included in this weighing, and it *is* lighter. Take two of those coins and weigh them against each other in Weighing 3. If they balance, the third coin is the counterfeit and it's lighter. If they don't balance, the lighter one is the counterfeit.
    *   **Case 2c: The scale tips (towards B, the original heavier side).** This means the counterfeit coin is one of the three original 'heavier' coins (from group B) included in this weighing, and it *is* heavier. Take two of those coins and weigh them against each other in Weighing 3. If they balance, the third coin is the counterfeit and it's heavier. If they don't balance, the heavier one is the counterfeit.

This solution, though complex, demonstrates the power of breaking a problem down into smaller, manageable parts.

## Puzzle 2: The Bridge and Torch Problem

**The Puzzle:**

Four people need to cross a bridge at night. They have only one torch, and the bridge can only hold two people at a time.  The people take different times to cross the bridge:

*   Person A: 1 minute
*   Person B: 2 minutes
*   Person C: 5 minutes
*   Person D: 8 minutes

When two people cross the bridge together, they must travel at the speed of the slower person. What is the minimum amount of time it takes for all four people to cross the bridge?

**Solution:**

This puzzle highlights the importance of strategic pairing. The intuitive approach of sending the two fastest people across each time isn't optimal. Here's the solution:

1.  A and B cross together (2 minutes).
2.  A returns with the torch (1 minute).
3.  C and D cross together (8 minutes).
4.  B returns with the torch (2 minutes).
5.  A and B cross together (2 minutes).

**Total time: 2 + 1 + 8 + 2 + 2 = 15 minutes**

Why does this work?  The key is to minimize the number of times the two slowest people (C and D) have to travel alone. Sending them together avoids one of them needing to cross separately.

## Puzzle 3: The Number Sequence Puzzle

**The Puzzle:**

What is the next number in the following sequence?

1, 11, 21, 1211, 111221, 312211, ________

**Solution:**

This isn't a typical mathematical sequence.  Instead, it's a descriptive sequence.  Each number describes the previous number.

*   1: "One 1"  ->  11
*   11: "Two 1s" -> 21
*   21: "One 2, one 1" -> 1211
*   1211: "One 1, one 2, two 1s" -> 111221
*   111221: "Three 1s, two 2s, one 1" -> 312211

Therefore, the next number in the sequence is **13112221** ("One 3, one 1, two 2s, two 1s").

## Puzzle 4: The Missing Number Puzzle

**The Puzzle:**

Find the missing number:

```
8 + 2 = 16106
5 + 3 = 15082
9 + 1 = 90108
7 + 4 = 28113
6 + 5 = ?
```

**Solution:**

This puzzle is about pattern recognition. The answer isn't a simple sum. The pattern is as follows:

*   First two digits: Multiply the two numbers.
*   Next two digits: Add the two numbers.
*   Last digit: Subtract the second number from the first number.

Therefore:

6 + 5 =

*   6 * 5 = 30
*   6 + 5 = 11
*   6 - 5 = 1

The missing number is **30111**

## Puzzle 5:  The Counterfeit Bill

**The Puzzle:**

A man walks into a store and buys a product that costs \$10. He pays with a \$100 bill. The store owner doesn't have change, so he goes next door to another store and gets change for the \$100 bill.  He then gives the man \$90 in change.  Later, the store owner from next door discovers that the \$100 bill was counterfeit, and he demands that the first store owner give him a real \$100 bill.  How much money did the first store owner lose?

**Solution:**

This puzzle is designed to be confusing with extraneous information. Here's a breakdown of the losses:

*   **\$10 (Cost of the Product):** The store owner effectively gave away the product for free.
*   **\$90 (Change Given):** The store owner had to give the man change for the fake bill.
*   **\$100 (Replaced the Counterfeit Bill):** The store owner had to reimburse the neighbor.

Therefore, the first store owner lost **\$100**.  The cost of the product is included within the \$100 because the store owner lost that potential profit. The important concept here is that the store owner *eventually* had to replace the counterfeit \$100 with a real \$100, making his loss equal to the bill's value.

##  Adding Some Code:  A Simple Number Guessing Game

Let's make things a bit interactive with a simple number guessing game implemented in JavaScript.  This isn't a puzzle in itself, but it reinforces logical thinking and number sense.

```javascript
import React, { useState } from 'react';

function NumberGuessingGame() {
  const [secretNumber, setSecretNumber] = useState(Math.floor(Math.random() * 100) + 1);
  const [guess, setGuess] = useState('');
  const [message, setMessage] = useState('');
  const [attempts, setAttempts] = useState(0);

  const handleGuessChange = (event) => {
    setGuess(event.target.value);
  };

  const handleGuessSubmit = () => {
    const guessedNumber = parseInt(guess, 10);

    if (isNaN(guessedNumber) || guessedNumber < 1 || guessedNumber > 100) {
      setMessage('Please enter a valid number between 1 and 100.');
      return;
    }

    setAttempts(attempts + 1);

    if (guessedNumber === secretNumber) {
      setMessage(`Congratulations! You guessed the number ${secretNumber} in ${attempts + 1} attempts!`);
      setSecretNumber(Math.floor(Math.random() * 100) + 1); // Reset game
      setAttempts(0);
    } else if (guessedNumber < secretNumber) {
      setMessage('Too low! Try again.');
    } else {
      setMessage('Too high! Try again.');
    }
  };

  return (
    <div>
      <h2>Number Guessing Game</h2>
      <p>I'm thinking of a number between 1 and 100. Can you guess it?</p>
      <input
        type="number"
        value={guess}
        onChange={handleGuessChange}
        placeholder="Enter your guess"
      />
      <button onClick={handleGuessSubmit}>Guess</button>
      <p>{message}</p>
    </div>
  );
}

export default NumberGuessingGame;
```
